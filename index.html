Understanding Your Income

How to use:

  • Add each income source via the Edit income & tax button and choose Salaried (tax estimated) or Self-employed (enter your net).
  • Mark your main earner as Primary — its tax bracket breakdown appears below.
  • Focus on the Net column — that's your actual take-home pay.
  • Check the stat cards at the bottom for your average pay per fortnight and month.
Learn more
  • Gross vs net pay: Your gross is what your employer puts on your contract. Net is what lands in your bank account after tax. This tool focuses on net — because that's the number you can actually spend or save.
  • How tax brackets actually work: Australia's system is progressive — you pay 0% on the first ~$18k, then rising rates on each extra slice. A pay rise that pushes you into a higher bracket only taxes the portion above the threshold, never your whole salary. Salaried sources estimate this for you; self-employed sources let you enter your net directly.
  • Superannuation explained: Your employer pays 11.5% of your ordinary earnings into super on top of your salary — it's not deducted from your gross. It's your money, invested for retirement, and understanding it early pays off massively.
  • Counting all your income: Beyond your main job, track any freelance, side gigs, investment dividends, or regular government payments. Each stream reduces your reliance on a single source.

Managing Your Expenses

How to use:

  • Add each expense via the Edit expenses button with its name, amount, and real frequency (weekly, monthly, yearly…).
  • Use the search bar to find a specific expense by name.
  • Check the stat cards below for your total weekly spend, annual run rate, and the Essential Ratio.
Learn more
  • Needs vs wants — a practical lens: Needs keep your life running (rent, utilities, groceries, minimum debt payments). Wants make life enjoyable (dining out, streaming, hobbies). This tool's Essential Ratio tells you what share of your spending is non-negotiable — a useful gut check.
  • Fixed vs variable — where you can actually adjust: Rent and loan repayments are fixed each month; groceries and entertainment are variable. When you need to cut back, start with variable non-essentials — you can cook more often, but you can't change your lease overnight.
  • Spending on yourself isn't the enemy: Cutting every joy to chase a savings number is unsustainable. The goal isn't deprivation — it's intention. Budget for the things that genuinely make you happy, cut the ones you don't even notice. A plan with room for joy is one you'll actually stick to.

Building Your Financial Future

How to use:

  • Set your FI multiple and expected return via the Edit assets, allocation & FI button — these drive your FI estimate.
  • Track your Savings Rate and Years to FI in the stat cards below.
  • Watch the FI progress to see how close you are to your target nest egg.
Learn more
  • Your savings rate is your most powerful lever: The share of net income you keep — not your salary, not your returns — is the single biggest driver of when you reach financial independence. A high earner who spends it all gets there slower than a moderate earner who saves aggressively.
  • Financial independence, defined: FI isn't necessarily quitting work forever. It's the point where your investments generate enough to cover your core living costs — giving you the freedom to work because you want to, not because you have to. The FI multiple you set is your personal target: how many years of expenses you want saved before you call it.
  • The 4% rule — a starting point, not a guarantee: A common guide says ~25× your annual expenses invested can sustain a long retirement. If you spend $50k a year, that's roughly $1.25M. It's a useful compass — but real returns vary, and your timeline matters. Treat it as a target to aim past, not a number you can retire on the day you hit it.
  • Compound growth — the quiet engine: Reinvested returns earn their own returns. A dollar saved at 30 can be worth multiples of a dollar saved at 50 — not because you earned more, but because it had longer to snowball. Saving early lets compounding do the heavy lifting over time.
  • Good saving habits that stick: Automate transfers the day you're paid — if you never see the money in your spending account, you won't miss it. Pick a percentage, not a dollar amount, so your savings grow with pay rises. Build a small emergency buffer first (a few months of essentials), then direct everything else toward your FI goal.
  • Where to put your savings — a quick menu: High-interest savings accounts for your emergency fund (safe, accessible, ~5% right now in Australia). Offset accounts against a mortgage (tax-free effective return at your loan rate). Broad-market ETFs for long-term FI investing (low-cost diversification). Superannuation — the tax-advantaged long game. Each has a different job; the right mix depends on your timeline and goals.

Understanding Your Liabilities

How to use:

  • Add each liability via the Edit liabilities button with its name, balance, and interest rate.
  • Check the Liability Summary card for each debt's monthly and annual interest cost.
  • Scroll to the Debt Reduction Impact card to see your Net Worth if Debt-Free.
Learn more
  • Good debt vs bad debt — a practical lens: Good debt builds assets or earns more than it costs (mortgage on an appreciating property, student loans that boost your income). Bad debt finances consumption at high interest (credit cards, car loans for more car than you need). The interest rate is the tell — if it's over ~8%, it's probably worth clearing fast.
  • Debt avalanche vs snowball — pick what works: Avalanche pays the highest interest rate first (math-optimal). Snowball pays the smallest balance first (momentum-optimal — quick wins keep you going). This tool shows each debt's monthly and annual interest cost, so you can see which one is bleeding you the most.
  • Interest compounds against you too: A $5,000 credit card at 20% costs ~$1,000 a year in interest if you only pay the minimum. That's money you could be saving instead. The Liability Summary card makes this visible — annual interest cost is the number to stare at.
  • Your net worth tells the whole story: Assets minus liabilities. You might have $50k saved, but if you owe $30k, your true position is $20k. The Net Worth if Debt-Free card shows what your finances look like without the drag of debt — that's the gap you're closing.

Financial Scenario Planning

How to use:

  • Edit any section below — income, expenses, assets, liabilities, allocation or FI — to model a change. Nothing here touches your real data.
  • Click Calculate Scenario to see a full simulated dashboard of that hypothetical.
  • Use the View period selector on the results to switch between weekly, fortnightly, monthly and yearly.
  • Click Reset to current to reload every section from your live data.
Learn more
  • What "what if" planning is really for: Big money decisions are hard to picture in your head. This tab lets you try one on for size — a pay rise, a move, a new car loan, a savings push — and see the knock-on effect on your savings, net worth and FI date before you commit a single real dollar.
  • It's a true sandbox: Every section here is a private copy of your finances. Add a source, delete an expense, pay down a debt — your real Dashboard, Savings and Liabilities don't budge. When you're done exploring, Reset to current wipes the slate back to reality.
  • The levers that move the needle most: Your FI date is driven by three things — how much you keep (income minus expenses), the return you assume, and your FI multiple. Small, sustainable changes usually beat dramatic ones: trimming a recurring expense or nudging your savings rate up a few points compounds harder over time than a one-off windfall.
  • Reading the deltas: Each result shows a ↑/↓ badge versus your current plan — green when the change is in a good direction (more savings, fewer years to FI), red when it isn't, and "no change" when a figure didn't move. They make trade-offs obvious: a raise that all goes to lifestyle shows income ↑ but FI flat.
  • Set goals on your buckets: Give an allocation bucket a current balance and a target, and the simulated dashboard shows how long it'll take to get there at the scenario's savings rate. It's the fastest way to answer "if I change X, when do I hit my $Y goal?"
  • Scenarios worth trying: A pay rise (and where it actually goes). Cutting your biggest non-essential. Clearing a high-interest debt. Shifting your allocation toward Grow. A more conservative return assumption — it's wise to check your plan still holds if markets underperform.

How the buckets work

This follows the Barefoot Investor (Scott Pape) three-bucket system: give every dollar of your take-home pay a job. Together the buckets cover daily life, your safety net, and your future.

  • Blow — day-to-day living. Pape suggests four sub-accounts:
    • Daily Expenses (60%) — everyday bills, groceries, transport
    • Splurge (10%) — guilt-free fun money
    • Smile (10%) — short-term savings (holidays, concerts, gadgets)
    • Fire Extinguisher (20%) — attacks debt first, then fills Mojo, then super, then flows into Grow
  • Mojo — safety-net cash. Start with $2,000, build to 3–6 months of expenses. Hands off unless it's a real emergency.
  • Grow — long-term wealth: super, shares, property, or a side business.

Make it your own — create whatever categories make sense for you. The only rule: your allocations must total 100%.
